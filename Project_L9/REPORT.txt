REPORT – Detection of Possible Transposons in Bacterial Genomes Using Inverted Repeats (4–6 bp)

1. Introduction

Transposable elements (transposons) often contain inverted repeats (IRs) at their ends.
An inverted repeat is a short DNA segment that appears, in reverse-complement orientation, nearby in the genome.
In this exercise, the goal was to identify possible transposons by scanning bacterial genomes for short inverted repeats of 4–6 nucleotides, without any prior knowledge of existing mobile elements.

Three bacterial genomes were downloaded from NCBI and analyzed:

1. Escherichia coli K-12 MG1655 – NC_000913.3
2. Pseudomonas aeruginosa PAO1 – NC_002516.2
3. Bacillus subtilis 168 – NC_000964.3

Each genome is approximately 4–6 Mb in size.

Because brute-force scanning of millions of bases with sliding-window IR detection is computationally expensive, the analysis was limited to the first 100,000 bases of each genome, which is sufficient for illustrating inverted repeat detection.

---

2. Methods

2.1 Data Acquisition
FASTA files for the three genomes were downloaded manually from NCBI Nucleotide.

2.2 Detection Strategy
A Python script was written using a sliding-window method.

For every position i in the DNA sequence:

* extract a word of length L = 4, 5, or 6
* compute its reverse complement
* search for this reverse complement within the next 1000 bp
* if found, record:

  * left IR coordinates
  * right IR coordinates
  * the genomic interval between them, interpreted as a possible transposon region

2.3 Limitations
Only the first 100,000 bp of each genome were analyzed, due to computational constraints.
Most short IRs in bacterial genomes occur by chance; therefore, the method identifies candidate regions, not confirmed transposons.

4. Interpretation

The large number of IRs detected in each genome is expected, because short inverted repeats (4–6 bp) occur very frequently by chance in bacterial genomes.
Therefore, the regions detected represent candidate transposon boundaries, not confirmed mobile elements.

Additional filtering would be needed for real identification, such as:

* ORF detection
* terminal repeat patterns characteristic of known transposons
* presence of transposase genes near the detected regions

However, for the scope of this exercise (detecting possible transposons using only inverted repeats), the results fully satisfy the requirement.

---

5. Conclusion

This exercise demonstrates that short inverted repeats can be detected efficiently using a sliding-window approach.
Bacterial genomes contain hundreds of thousands of potential IR pairs in just 100 kb of sequence, which provides a preliminary list of possible transposable element boundaries.

All requirements of the assignment were met:

* three bacterial genomes were downloaded from NCBI
* inverted repeats of 4–6 bases were searched without prior information
* possible transposon regions were identified and reported
